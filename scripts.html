Cold calling scripts for loan officers — the realtor edition

Written by a working LO who makes these calls. No "smile and dial" fluff — these are the openers, voicemails, and objection answers for one specific job: turning real estate agents into repeat referral partners.

Before you dial: the 30-second edge

The difference between a cold call and a warm one is one piece of real information. Before you dial, know one of: their latest closing, their listing inventory, whether they work buyers or listings, or something they posted this week. Open with that, and you're not a telemarketer — you're a colleague who did their homework.

Opener: the listing agent

"Hey [Name], this is [You] — I'm a loan officer here in [market]. I saw your listing on [street] — congrats, that one's going to move. Quick reason for the call: when buyers come through your open houses without financing lined up, who do you usually point them to?"
Then be quiet. The answer tells you everything: no lender (opportunity), a lender they love (long game), or a lender they tolerate (wedge).

Opener: the buyer's agent

"Hey [Name], [You] with [Company]. I work with a lot of buyers in [market] and I keep seeing your name on the buy side — clearly you're doing something right. Honest question: what's the most frustrating thing your lender did to you on your last deal?"
Every buyer's agent has a lender horror story. Let them tell it — then position against it: speed, communication, weekend pre-approvals.

The denied-buyer rescue (highest-converting angle)

"One more thing before I let you go — when a buyer gets declined and the deal dies, what happens to them? ... Here's my pitch: send me your dead deals. Credit issues, self-employed income, bank-statement situations. If I can revive even one of those a quarter, that's a commission you'd already written off."
You're not asking them to switch lenders — you're asking for deals that are worth $0 to them today. Zero risk to say yes.

Voicemail (under 20 seconds)

"Hey [Name], [You], local loan officer. I had a thought about your listing on [street] that takes 90 seconds. I'll try you again [day] — or grab me at [number]. Again, that's [You] at [number]."
One specific hook, one callback window, number twice. Never pitch in voicemail.

Past the front desk

"Hi — [You] for [Agent]. It's about one of their listings."
True (you're calling about their listing), short, and it gets transferred. Don't pitch the gatekeeper.

The four objections you'll hear every day

"I already have a lender."

"Of course you do — every good agent does. I'm not asking to replace them. I'm asking to be the person you call when they're slow, when it's a weekend, or when they say no. Backup quarterback. Costs you nothing until the day it saves a deal."

"Just send me an email."

"Happy to — and I know where those emails go 🙂 Tell you what: I'll send one line with my cell in it. If your lender ever leaves you hanging on a Saturday pre-approval, that's the number. Fair?"

"I'm busy."

"Totally fair — 20 seconds: I'm a local LO, I return calls in minutes not days, and I'd love to earn one shot at impressing you. When's a better time this week for 5 minutes?"

"How'd you get my number?"

"You're on [source — your brokerage site / your listing on MLS]. I only call agents who are actually producing — that's the compliment hidden in this cold call."

The cadence: 6 touches, 3 weeks

Industry training repeats it for a reason: an agent becomes a partner after 6–8 meaningful touches, not one great call. The cadence that works:

TouchDayWhat
1Day 1Cold call (scripts above)
2Day 1Text or email recap — one line + your cell
3Day 4Value touch: a rate scenario or a buyer-ready flyer for their listing
4Day 8Call #2 — reference the value touch, ask for coffee
5Day 14Comment/engage on their social or congratulate a closing
6Day 21Call #3 — the direct ask: "send me one dead deal"
✓ The deals die in the gaps between touches. If you're tracking this in your head or a spreadsheet, the cadence is fiction by Thursday. (This is literally why PitchLog exists — every call logs itself and the follow-up books itself.)

Two compliance lines that keep you safe

⚠️ Recording: some states (Florida, California, and ~10 more) require everyone on the call to consent to recording. The professional fix: open with "I'm on a recorded line." Cell phones: calling an agent's published business line about business is generally DNC-exempt, but keep dialing human-initiated — autodialing cell phones is where TCPA lawsuits live.
