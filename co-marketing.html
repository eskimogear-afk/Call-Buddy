RESPA-safe co-marketing: what loan officers can actually do with realtors

You're cold-calling agents and offering value. Good. But "value" is exactly the word RESPA Section 8 cares about — and the line between smart partnership and a federal violation is thinner than most LO coaching admits. Plain English, no legalese:

The one-sentence rule

You cannot give a real estate agent anything of value in exchange for referrals. Not money, not leads, not paying their marketing bills, not gift cards, not event tickets tied to who sent you business. And there is no "small stuff" exception — RESPA has no de minimis threshold. A $25 gift card for a referral is the same legal theory as a $25,000 marketing agreement for referrals.

What it costs when it goes wrong

In August 2023 the CFPB hit Freedom Mortgage with a $1.75 million penalty over exactly the playbook some coaches still teach: the lender paid ~$90,000/month across dozens of brokerages under "marketing services agreements," and the CFPB found the agreements were really payment for referrals — the required marketing services largely never happened. The brokerage side got fined too. That was the CFPB's loudest RESPA enforcement in years, and it was aimed squarely at LO-agent co-marketing.

🚫 Stays on the wrong side of the line

  • Paying any share of an agent's Zillow/realtor.com placement, postcards, or ads because they refer you buyers
  • MSAs where the "marketing services" are vague, undocumented, or never delivered
  • Splitting open-house costs where your share exceeds the fair value of your own exposure
  • Gift cards, bonuses, raffle entries, or "lunch budgets" tied to referral volume
  • Free CRM access, lead lists, or software you pay for, handed to referring agents
  • Desk rental "agreements" priced above market because of the referral flow

✓ Generally safe — because no value transfers for referrals

  • Co-branded open-house flyers where you pay only for your own block of the flyer (your financing info, your NMLS) and it markets to the general public
  • Educational content — first-time-buyer guides, payment one-pagers — co-branded and distributed publicly
  • Being genuinely fast: same-day pre-approvals, weekend availability, denied-buyer rescues. Service isn't a thing of value under §8 — it's the product
  • Sitting open houses where you bring your own materials and answer financing questions for visitors
  • Coffee, a normal-priced lunch, and marketing where each party pays fair market value for their own share

The test to run before any joint spend

  1. Would I do this for an agent who never sent me a deal? If no, it's probably payment for referrals.
  2. Is my payment ≤ the fair market value of what I'm getting (my actual ad space, my actual exposure)? Document the math.
  3. Is it aimed at the public, not at the agent's referral decision?
  4. Is it in writing, with the services actually performed and receipts kept?
⚠️ This guide is education, not legal advice. Co-marketing arrangements deserve 30 minutes with a RESPA attorney before money moves — that consult costs less than the first hour of an enforcement response.
